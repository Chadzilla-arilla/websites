Understanding FDA Recall Classifications

Published September 15, 2025 ‚Ä¢ 8 min read

When the FDA issues a product recall, it assigns a classification that indicates the level of health hazard the product poses. Understanding these classifications can help you make informed decisions about recalled products and assess the urgency of the situation.

Recall Classifications

The FDA uses a three-tier system to classify recalls based on the severity of the health risk:

Class I Recall

HIGH RISK

Most Serious: Products that pose a reasonable probability of serious adverse health consequences or death.

Examples:

  • Food contaminated with deadly bacteria (e.g., Listeria, E. coli)
  • Medical devices with life-threatening defects
  • Medications with incorrect active ingredients
  • Infant formula with dangerous contamination

Action Required: Stop using immediately and seek medical attention if you've been exposed.

Class II Recall

MODERATE RISK

Moderate Risk: Products that may cause temporary or medically reversible adverse health consequences.

Examples:

  • Food with undeclared allergens
  • Medications with incorrect dosage instructions
  • Medical devices with non-life-threatening defects
  • Cosmetics with harmful ingredients

Action Required: Stop using the product and consult healthcare provider if needed.

Class III Recall

LOW RISK

Least Serious: Products unlikely to cause adverse health consequences but violate FDA regulations.

Examples:

  • Food with minor labeling errors
  • Products with incorrect net weight statements
  • Cosmetics with unapproved color additives
  • Minor packaging defects

Action Required: Use your judgment; health risk is minimal but product may not meet standards.

Recall Status Stages

Recalls progress through several status stages as the FDA monitors compliance and effectiveness:

1

Ongoing

Recall is active and the company is working to remove products from the market. Consumers should stop using the product immediately.

2

Pending

FDA is reviewing the recall plan or waiting for additional information from the company. Precautionary measures should still be taken.

3

Completed

The company has successfully removed or corrected all recalled products from the market to FDA's satisfaction.

4

Terminated

FDA has determined the recall is no longer necessary, often because the product is past its shelf life or no longer in commerce.

Additional Status Types

  • Voluntary: Company initiates the recall voluntarily
  • FDA Requested: FDA requests the company to initiate a recall
  • FDA Mandated: FDA legally requires the recall (rare, used when voluntary efforts fail)

What to Do During a Recall

  1. Stop using the product immediately if it's Class I or II
  2. Check the lot numbers, dates, and other identifying information against your products
  3. Follow disposal instructions provided in the recall notice
  4. Contact your healthcare provider if you've used a recalled product and have health concerns
  5. Report adverse events to the FDA through their reporting systems
  6. Check for refunds or replacements as companies often provide remedies
